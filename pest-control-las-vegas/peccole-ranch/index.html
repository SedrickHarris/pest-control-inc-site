PECCOLE RANCH, WEST LAS VEGAS NV 89117 / 89128

A Lake-Anchored West Las Vegas Master-Planned Community

Peccole Ranch is a master-planned community in west Las Vegas near Summerlin South, in ZIP codes 89117 and 89128. The community was built out across the closing decades of the twentieth century, and homes and infrastructure are now well-aged — that age creates the kind of gaps, plumbing wear, and slab conditions that newer construction does not have. The housing mix is a defining feature too: condos, townhomes, and single-family homes all share the same blocks and HOA infrastructure, and that multi-unit density accelerates pest spread once an infestation gets started.

The lakes and water features on Peccole Ranch property are the largest single pest driver. Persistent standing water in an otherwise arid desert climate creates year-round moisture gradients in the surrounding soil and landscaping. Argentine ants, German and American cockroaches, earwigs, and other moisture-seeking species are drawn to those moisture corridors at elevated rates compared to drier inland Las Vegas neighborhoods. Water also attracts birds and supports the rodent populations that nest in the community's mature trees.

Decades of maturing landscaping have produced a thick canopy of trees and shrubs across the community — particularly palms and fruit-bearing species — that serves as roof rat nesting habitat and ant foraging corridors. The Summerlin-adjacent location adds some scorpion pressure from the western desert edge, though density is lower than for communities sitting directly on the Summerlin perimeter. Effective treatment in Peccole Ranch is recurring, exterior-first, and tuned for moisture-driven pest activity.

The Health Conscious Service Program is exterior-first and tuned for water-adjacent and HOA-governed communities like Peccole Ranch — eco-friendly, family safe, and pet safe.

PECCOLE RANCH PEST PRESSURE

Top Pests in Peccole Ranch, NV

The pests that drive the most service calls in Peccole Ranch are tied directly to the community's defining features — lakes and water features, decades of mature landscaping, and the mix of multi-unit and single-family housing. Each species below is treated under the Health Conscious Service Program.

Argentine & Pavement Ants

Argentine ants are the dominant ant species in Peccole Ranch. Lakes and water features create persistent soil moisture that sustains large Argentine ant colonies year-round, and HOA common areas with irrigated landscaping provide high-density foraging corridors that connect directly to residential structures.

German & American Cockroaches

Cockroaches exploit the moisture gradients created by lakes, irrigation systems, and aging plumbing in the community's older multi-unit and single-family structures. German and American cockroach populations move freely through shared walls and utility corridors in condos and townhomes.

Roof Rats & Norway Rats

Roof rats have benefited from decades of maturing vegetation in Peccole Ranch. Palms and fruit-bearing trees serve as nesting sites and travel corridors into attics. Norway rats exploit moisture zones near lake edges and irrigation equipment throughout the community.

Subterranean Termites

Active year-round in Clark County soil. Peccole Ranch's aging slab foundations, many built before modern sub-slab pre-treatment standards became routine, carry elevated termite risk. Moisture from lakes and irrigation can amplify subterranean termite activity in adjacent soil zones.

Arizona Bark Scorpions

Bark scorpions are active throughout Clark County. In Peccole Ranch they are found in block walls, garages, and areas of dense vegetation and ground cover. Pressure is lower than in communities directly on the Summerlin or desert perimeter, but it is not absent and is treated under the same program.

Earwigs & Other Moisture Pests

Earwigs, silverfish, and other moisture-loving species are amplified by the lake and water-feature environment. They harbor in irrigated landscape beds, mulch zones, and under landscape lighting fixtures along the lake and common-area perimeters.
