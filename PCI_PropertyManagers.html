TENANT PEST COMPLAINTS · NEVADA LAW · RESPONSE PROTOCOL · DOCUMENTATION

Tenant Pest Complaints — The Response Protocol That Protects Your Compliance

Under Nevada's implied warranty of habitability (NRS 118A.290), Las Vegas landlords and property managers must respond to tenant pest complaints within a reasonable timeframe — generally 14 to 30 days for written notice, faster for urgent conditions like scorpions or bed bugs. Failure to document your response creates habitability liability. Pest Control Inc active property management accounts receive priority dispatch — and every visit produces the written service record that closes the compliance loop.

Acknowledge complaint same day in writing Dispatch Pest Control Inc within 24–48 hours Confirm service date with tenant in writing Provide 24-hour advance notice before unit entry Receive written service record same day as visit Follow up with tenant 5–7 days post-service

What Nevada Law Requires

NRS 118A.290 requires rental units to be maintained in habitable condition including freedom from pest infestation. Written tenant notice triggers your response obligation. Pest Control Inc provides the dispatch and the documentation — you provide the written acknowledgment and the scheduled service date.

What PCI Provides

Same-day or next-day dispatch for active accounts. Written service record delivered after every visit — unit number, pest type, treatment applied, products used, technician name, and date. Your compliance trail is complete from complaint to resolution.

What This Protects

A documented response and a written service record protect you against NRS 118A habitability claims, security deposit disputes, and lease termination actions. Without documentation, a tenant's word against yours is a liability. With documentation, the complaint is closed.

A property management pest control program is a portfolio-wide service agreement between a property manager and a licensed pest control vendor — covering all units, all zones, all properties in the portfolio under a single program with per-unit volume pricing, consolidated invoicing, and uniform documentation. It differs from individual unit service in that it treats the portfolio as the unit of service, not the individual apartment. A complete program covers occupied units on a recurring schedule, vacant units for turnover certification, common areas monthly, exterior and building perimeters, and emergency complaint response — all documented in a format that supports habitability compliance under Nevada law.
Property management pest control differs from residential pest control in five specific ways. First, pricing — property management programs use per-unit volume pricing across the portfolio, not per-property residential rates. Second, documentation — every visit produces a written service record designed for habitability compliance files, not a homeowner receipt. Third, compliance — programs are structured around Nevada NRS 118A landlord obligations and tenant notification requirements. Fourth, scope — programs cover all zones of the building, not a single household. Fifth, workflow — tenant communication protocols, complaint response timelines, and vendor onboarding documentation are standard features of a property management program, not available from a residential pest control vendor.

The five highest-pressure pests in Las Vegas rental properties are:
  • German cockroaches — the defining pest of multifamily buildings, spreading through shared walls, plumbing chases, and laundry infrastructure; require IGR and gel bait programs, not spray treatment
  • Bed bugs — introduced by tenants and their belongings; require unit-by-unit detection protocols
  • Rodents (roof rats and Norway rats) — enter through exterior gaps and travel through mechanical spaces and wall voids
  • Bark scorpions — endemic to the Las Vegas desert climate; especially problematic in properties adjacent to desert terrain
  • Ants — the most common complaint pest at the building perimeter and in common areas

Secondary pest pressure in Las Vegas rental properties includes flies in trash and laundry areas, silverfish in storage, and occasional wasps at exterior lighting.
